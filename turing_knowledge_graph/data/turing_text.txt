Alan Mathison Turing was born on 23 June 1912 in Maida Vale, London, England.
He was a British mathematician, computer scientist, logician, cryptanalyst, philosopher, and theoretical biologist.
Turing studied mathematics at King's College, Cambridge, where he was elected a Fellow in 1935.
He then pursued a PhD at Princeton University under Alonzo Church, completing it in 1938.

In 1936, Turing published his landmark paper "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem",
in which he introduced the concept of the Turing Machine — a theoretical model of computation capable of simulating
any algorithmic process. This work laid the foundations of computer science and computability theory.

During World War II, Turing worked at Bletchley Park, the centre of British cryptanalysis and signals intelligence.
He joined the Government Code and Cypher School (GC&CS) and made vital contributions to breaking the Enigma cipher
used by Nazi Germany. Working alongside Gordon Welchman, Hugh Alexander, and Max Newman, Turing designed the
electromechanical Bombe, which greatly accelerated the decryption of Enigma messages.
His wartime work is estimated to have shortened the war by two to four years.

After the war, Turing worked at the National Physical Laboratory (NPL) in London, where he designed the Automatic
Computing Engine (ACE), one of the earliest stored-program computer designs.
In 1948, he moved to the University of Manchester to work on the Manchester Mark 1 computer.

In 1950, Turing published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" in the journal Mind, posing the question
"Can machines think?" and proposing the Turing Test as a measure of machine intelligence.
This paper is widely regarded as the founding document of Artificial Intelligence research.

In 1952, Turing published "The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis" in the Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society,
describing a mathematical model for how biological patterns (such as animal stripes and spots) arise through
reaction-diffusion systems. This work founded the field of mathematical biology.

Turing was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) in 1951, and was awarded an OBE in 1946
for his services during World War II.

Alan Turing died on 7 June 1954 in Wilmslow, Cheshire, England, at the age of 41.
In 2009, British Prime Minister Gordon Brown issued a formal apology on behalf of the British government
for the persecution Turing suffered due to his homosexuality.
In 2013, Queen Elizabeth II granted Turing a posthumous royal pardon.
Since 2021, Turing's portrait has appeared on the British fifty pound note.
The annual ACM Turing Award, often called the Nobel Prize of Computing, was named in his honour.
